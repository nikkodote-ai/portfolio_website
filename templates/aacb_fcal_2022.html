INTRODUCTION

Differentiation of IBS from IBD is important as they have different treatment strategies and a non-invasive way to accomplish this is

by measuring faecal calprotectin.


Calprotectin is a calcium- and zinc-binding protein found in neutrophils and excreted in faeces; it serves as a marker neutrophilic intestinal inflammation.


We were previously using POCT Quantum blue fCAL extended assay (lateral flow assay) which was simple but labour-intensive and time-consuming.


To improve overall performance without compromising on quality, we verified the use of the BÃ¼hlmann fCal turbo assay using the Optilite automated benchtop analyser.


Internal quality control (IQC) material provided with the fCAL turbo reagent kit at low and high concentrations, and two samples containing calprotectin from the INSTAND (DÃ¼sseldorf, Germany) external quality assurance scheme for faecal markers, were analysed in quintuplicate on the same day and on different days (N = 5) to ascertain accuracy and imprecision

The lower limit of quantitation (LLoQ) was assessed by appropriate dilutions (non-serial) of an extracted patient sample (calprotectin concentration approximately 200 Âµg/g) with Calex Cap stool extraction buffer, using an acceptance criterion of <10% for inaccuracy and imprecision (mean of triplicates). Faecal extraction is the same for both methods.

RESULTS

There was a good agreement (Rs = 0.96) and no significant bias (-6.7 %, p = 0.10) present between the fCAL turbo and Quantum Blue extended assays (Figure 1 and 2).


Intra- and inter-assay imprecision was <5 %. Inaccuracy is less than 10 %, except for the Low QC inter-assay which is 13 %.


Calprotectin concentrations were lower when measured by the fCAL turbo assay on the Optilite (median calprotectin 261, range 39â2,109 Âµg/g) compared to the Quantum Blue assay (median calprotectin 286, range 31â2,650 Âµg/g), but this difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.10).


The LLoQ is 25 Âµg/g and the assay is linear from 25 to 10,000 Âµg/g.


Sample carry-over was <1%


The fCAL turbo assay was calibrated once at the beginning of the verification and was not required again as ascertained by acceptable IQC results. This indicated an on-board calibration curve stability of up to 4 weeks.





Total analysis time for a batch of 30 patient samples after sample extraction was 15 and 35 minutes using the fCAL extended assay on the BÃ¼hlmann Quantum Blue Reader and the BÃ¼hlmann fCAL turbo assay on the Optilite, respectively. However, the hands-on time taken by the scientist to load samples for the BÃ¼hlmann fCAL turbo assay was < 5 minutes compared to the full 35 minutes for the BÃ¼hlmann Quantum Blue assay.


Using a subset of data on concentrations less than 50 to 300 Âµg/g, a comparative and descriptive statistical analysis (Figure 3 and 4) was performed to see how the method of interest performs in a clinically significant range for screening (<50â200 Âµg/g) and monitoring (<100â300 Âµg/g). There was a good correlation, and the bias was not statistically significant (p-value = 0.14, n = 19).

Discussion & Conclusion


The fCAL turbo assay on the Optilite compared very well with the Quantum Blue fCAL extended assay.

The benefits included shorter batch analysis and turn-around times, significant saving on scientist hands-on time, no manual transcription and automatic re-analysis of those samples requiring dilution. Furthermore, we found the calibration curve to be stable for up to 4-weeks allowing even greater savings in terms of not only operator time, but also reagent consumption.

No significant carryover which can be attributed to sample processing (500-fold dilution and centrifugation) and use of

special wash with extra wash on Optilite was identified.
